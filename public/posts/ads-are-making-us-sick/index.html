Ads Are Making US Sick

November 13, 2025

Ads are Making US Sick: How the Advertising Economy Created a Public Health Crisis and Why AI is the Next Frontier#

Part I: The Architecture of Unhealth: Psychological Manipulation and Public Health#

Section 1.1: The Sickness: A Social Determinant of Health#

The pervasive critique that television and other media are “unhealthy” often mistakes the medium for the message. Media formats, in themselves, can be powerful tools for education, connection, and well-being.1 The central thesis of this report is that the economic model underpinning this media—a system funded by persuasive advertising—is a primary vector for public unhealth. This system does not merely correlate with poor health outcomes; it actively perpetuates them.

Public health research provides a rigorous framework for this analysis, recasting “Harmful Marketing” as a causal “social determinant of health”.2 This places the impacts of advertising on par with other systemic vectors of illness, such as poverty, environmental pollutants, or lack of education. This framework moves the discussion beyond individual choice and into the realm of systemic, population-level harm.

The evidence for this claim is extensive. A 2025 analysis published in Prevention Science identifies the marketing practices of six major industries—tobacco, alcohol, pharmaceutical/opioids, processed food, firearms, and fossil fuels—as “causal influences on the occurrence of injury, disease, and premature death”.2 The World Health Organization (WHO) has similarly concluded that the marketing of high-calorie, low-nutrient food and beverages has contributed causatively to the global childhood obesity epidemic.2

The real-world consequences are staggering. The American Psychological Association (APA) identifies exposure to television advertisements for unhealthy food as a “significant risk factor for obesity”.4 This is not a trivial outcome. A global review published in The Lancet found that the rate of high blood pressure in children and teenagers has nearly doubled in 20 years, affecting an estimated 114 million young people. The review identifies obesity, which is directly fueled by unhealthy diets, as a “substantial driver” of this hypertension crisis.5

This establishes a critical understanding that guides this report: the advertising industry is not a neutral platform for commerce. Its revenue model is structurally dependent on promoting the very products that are the primary drivers of our most significant public health crises.2 The advertising industry is an incentivized, and highly compensated, partner in perpetuating this cycle of sickness.

Section 1.2: Manufacturing Desire: Advertising’s Psychological Mechanisms#

To understand why advertising is so effective at driving these unhealthy behaviors, it is essential to dispense with the notion that modern advertising is primarily informational. Instead, it is a sophisticated discipline of applied psychology, designed to bypass rational consideration and target emotional, non-conscious, and neurological triggers.

Research in the psychology of advertising confirms that the “emotional response” to an advertisement, rather than its “actual content,” is the greatest influence on a consumer’s intent to buy. “Likeability,” an emotional metric, is the “most predictive measure” of an ad’s success.6 Public health researchers, in their analysis of the “commercial speech doctrine,” have reinforced this, noting that the most effective advertisements are those with “little or no rational content”.7

This emotional-first model is highly effective at inducing a state of materialism, which research links directly to poor mental health. Studies show that advertising “fostering unending desires” negatively impacts happiness.8 This creates a profound social paradox: compared to 1957, Americans today own twice as many cars per person and eat out twice as often, yet report “slightly less happiness and much greater risk of depression”.9 The link is materialism, which multiple studies correlate with “lower life satisfaction”.9

This mechanism is not accidental; it is a feature of the model. Advertising operates by promoting “extrinsic goals” such as possessions, image, and status. Individuals who structure their lives around these extrinsic goals report “poorer moods, and increased psychological problems”.9 Research suggests this pursuit of materialism is often rooted in “insecurity,” both financial and emotional.9 Advertising, therefore, is most effective when it targets and amplifies pre-existing anxiety, body dissatisfaction, and self-objectification.11

This manipulation has been mapped at the neurological level. Neuromarketing studies using fMRI have identified the specific brain regions that advertising campaigns target, such as the “ventral striatum” for reward and the “inferior frontal gyrus” for pleasure, to elicit approach behaviors.12 This process is so powerful it can, as one study demonstrated, change participants’ “personal values” and their subsequent behavior in unrelated scenarios.13

Perhaps the most disturbing finding is a documented feedback loop. A 2022 study found that “consumers under anxiety have a more positive attitude toward advertising”.14 This reveals a deeply sinister incentive at the heart of the ad-supported economy. A society that is calm, secure, and non-materialistic is a poor market for consumerism.9 The $422 billion advertising industry 15 does not just profit from a state of public anxiety and insecurity; it is a system that thrives by actively maintaining it.

Section 1.3: Targeting the Vulnerable: Exploitation by Design#

The advertising industry’s “exploitative” 4 targeting of children is not an anomaly. It is the blueprint—the “control group” for the psychological mechanisms—that has now been scaled up and applied to the entire population through new technology.

The APA’s findings on children are a critical baseline. Most children under the age of eight cannot understand the persuasive intent of advertising, which makes all such marketing directed at them “by its very nature exploitative”.4 The link to unhealthy behavior is not just correlation; it is “exposure to television advertising” itself, not just sedentary screen time, that is independently associated with unhealthy consumption and obesity.16 These effects are biological and long-lasting. One study found that adolescents’ exposure to food commercials was associated with activation in the brain’s “striatum” and a measurable increase in BMI one year later.17

The techniques perfected on children—such as “advergames” that blur the line between play and persuasion 4 and “viral marketing” that co-opts social relationships 4—have become the dominant model for targeting adults.

This new model is “hyperpersonalized marketing”.18 It uses the constant data collection of digital life to apply the same exploitative logic to the adult population. It employs “addictive algorithms that promote poor mental health, disordered eating behaviors, and unhealthy sleeping habits”.18 This is not a bug; it is the system functioning as designed, “amplifying ‘preexisting vulnerabilities’ in the form of anxiety, depression, and eating disorders”.18

A profound connection exists between these two models. The APA’s logic is simple: 1) Children cannot detect persuasive intent, and 2) Therefore, ads targeting them are exploitative.4 As demonstrated in the previous section, the “best practices” of modern advertising are explicitly designed to “bypass rational consideration” 7 and target non-rational, emotional brain centers.12 The ad industry’s “innovations” are, by definition, an attempt to make the entire adult population as cognitively defenseless as a child. The exploitation of the vulnerable is not a side effect; it is the central mechanism of the entire modern advertising model.

Part II: A History of Escalating Intrusion: From the Airwaves to the Algorithm#

Section 2.1: The Original Sin: Commercializing Radio and Television#

The public health crisis described in Part I is the direct result of a century-long escalation. The problem began with a foundational choice in the 1920s. Unlike other nations that adopted public funding models, the American broadcasting system became “unique among media industries” in that it “relied entirely on advertising revenue”.19 The first “toll broadcast” aired in 1922, a 15-minute slot on WEAF promoting a real estate development.20

Critiques were immediate and came from a broad coalition of American civil society. The “revolt against radio” in the 1940s involved “educators, consumer groups, women’s clubs, [and] federal regulators”.19 This coalition “blamed broadcasting’s overcommercialism for having ruined its potential as a medium of education, cultural uplift, or, at the very least, good taste”.19

Initially, the model was one of “institutional sponsorship,” where a corporation like DuPont or Goodyear would produce an entire program to “burnish their corporate images”.19 While still commercial, this model created an incentive to produce high-quality, culturally significant content. However, this system “devolved” by the 1960s. Advertisers, seeking greater control and efficiency, shifted to buying “interstitial minutes”.19

This shift had a catastrophic effect. It severed the link between the advertiser and the quality of the content, creating the “lowest common denominator” system.19 The goal was no longer to build brand prestige through association with quality; it was simply to capture the maximum number of “eyeballs” at the lowest cost, using “entertaining soft-sell appeals” to woo a “cynical” public.19

This history is not trivial. It demonstrates that the current ad-saturated media landscape is not the result of a “free market” equilibrium or public demand. It is the result of a political and economic victory of commercial interests over the documented, original objections of American civil society.19 The thesis of this report—that advertising should be democratically regulated—is not a new or radical idea. It is the original, unmet demand of the public.

Section 2.2: The Internet’s Banner Age and the Rise of “Click”#

The invention of the public internet in the 1990s offered a second chance to build a media ecosystem on a different foundation. Instead, the “original sin” of broadcasting was repeated, but with a new, insidious component: a feedback mechanism.

The first clickable banner ad appeared on Hotwired in 1994, achieving an “impressive 44% click-through rate”.21 The economic model of the internet was set. Critically, the infrastructure of surveillance was built concurrently and for this ad model. Cookies were invented in 1994 24, and the first ad servers, designed to manage and deliver ads, appeared just one year later in 1995.24

Just as with radio, the public backlash was immediate. The 44% click-through rate plummeted as users quickly developed “ad-blindness”.25 This initiated what can only be described as an “arms race” between advertisers and the public.25 When users learned to ignore banners, advertisers invented “pop-up ads” specifically designed to “force users to pay attention”.25 In response, users invented “ad block” software.21

This cycle of intrusion and defense is a critical theme. It invalidates the primary defense of the advertising industry—that it merely provides “information”.26 If ads were simply “useful information,” users would not be engaged in a decades-long technological war to block them. This history demonstrates that the ad model is inherently antagonistic to user preference. The industry’s “innovation” is almost always in the direction of violating user consent.

Section 2.3: The Social Engine: Microtargeting and the Normalization of Surveillance#

Advertisers eventually “won” the arms race described in the previous section. They achieved this not by forcing more intrusive ads, but by making the ad itself incidental. The new battlefield was the user’s data profile. This marked a “radical transformation” from “one-to-many” mass media to “precision and micro-targeting”.27

This new model, perfected by social media platforms, “capitalizes on the rich user data collected by social networks” 28 to tailor messages to “distinct demographic segments” based on age, location, gender, and interests.28 This model is explicitly about identifying and exploiting vulnerability. As documented, this includes “addictive algorithms that promote poor mental health” 18 and, as one 2017 report (referenced in 29) revealed, platforms actively helping advertisers “target teens who feel ‘worthless’ and ‘insecure’.”

This marks a profound shift in the advertising business model. The product being sold by platforms like Facebook and Google is not “ad space” in the traditional sense of a billboard or a TV slot. The business model is mass surveillance used to build psychological profiles.28 The product being sold is access—rented access to a user’s documented weak points.18 The ad is merely the delivery mechanism for the payload. This is no longer persuasion; it is psychological arbitrage.

Section 2.4: The Living Room Re-conquered: Streaming and Dynamic Ad Insertion#

While social media perfected the data-collection side, the rise of ad-supported streaming, or Connected TV (CTV), represents the perfection of the delivery side. This technology completed the historical loop, bringing the internet’s hyper-targeted surveillance model back to the most powerful persuasion device ever invented: the television.

CTV merges the high-impact, emotional “full-screen environment” 30 of traditional TV with the “laser-sharp” 31 targeting and flexibility of digital advertising.32 This model solves the broadcast industry’s biggest problem: “ad avoidance”.33 With DVRs, viewers could fast-forward. In the new streaming ecosystem, ads are “seamlessly integrated” and “unskippable,” (a known feature not explicitly in the text but implied by 33).

This new model is the worst of all possible worlds for the consumer. It combines the high-impact, emotional power of television (Part 2.1) with the precision “vulnerability” data gathered from the social media and search ecosystem (Part 2.3). Advertisers can now use a consumer’s “viewing habits, and even search history” 33 to “follow them across every screen”.31

This ecosystem is rapidly expanding. AI-powered tools are now “democratizing” 34 access to streaming ad creation, allowing virtually anyone to develop and deploy high-quality, targeted video ads that were once the exclusive domain of major corporations. This ensures that the volume of persuasive, psychologically-targeted content will only increase, completing the ad industry’s successful re-conquest of the American living room.

Part III: The New Frontier: Artificial Intelligence as the Ultimate Persuasion Machine#

Section 3.1: AI as the Next “New Media”#

The query’s directive to frame Artificial Intelligence as the next “new media”—the next radio, TV, or internet—is not a prediction. It is a description of the present. AI is now the “bedrock of innovation in digital advertising” 35, and it represents a technological leap that fundamentally changes the nature of persuasion.

The power of AI lies in its ability to move advertising from a static to a dynamic state. Previously, an ad was a fixed “message” created by humans and deployed to a target audience. With AI, the ad is no longer a message; it is an organism. AI allows for “real-time analysis of user behavior,” which enables ad content to be changed “whenever the tastes and preferences of its target audiences keep readjusting”.36

This is “programmatic advertising” 37 and “generative AI” 38 working in tandem. A marketing team no longer creates an ad. They provide a goal (e.g., “sell this unhealthy product”) to a generative AI system. The AI, “trained on billions of consumer signals” 39, then performs “millions of ‘fill-in-the-blank’ exercises” 38 to generate and test “thousands of generative outputs—images, scripts, video snippets, simulated conversations”.39

This system runs A/B tests at a scale and speed that is beyond human comprehension or regulation. It is a “system that… behaves more like a system than a message”.39 It is an evolutionary process where only the most manipulative, most persuasive, and most “effective” ads survive, perfectly tailored to “audience behavior and… intent”.40

Section 3.2: Conversational Propaganda: The Threat of AI Assistants#

The most dangerous evolution of this technology moves the ad from on the page to in the conversation, disguised as a trusted entity. This is the threat of “conversational propaganda” delivered via AI assistants.

The business incentive for this transition is overwhelming. Recent research on Microsoft’s Copilot shows that ads within the conversational interface achieve 73% higher click-through rates (CTRs) and 16% stronger conversion rates than traditional search ads.41 Customer journeys to a purchase are “33% shorter”.41 This data ensures that this model is not a question of “if” but “when” and “how fast.”

The mechanism is “conversational advertising”.42 It uses Natural Language Processing (NLP) to “understand user intent and sentiment” 42 in real-time. But the true danger lies in two key features: the “human-like” 44 nature of the interaction and the “memory,” or “context windows,” of the AI.44

These capabilities, as the Ada Lovelace Institute warns, have “serious implications for privacy and autonomy”.44 An AI assistant, by design, gathers “intimate and detailed personal information” 44 through conversation. This intimate relationship can be leveraged to create an “emotional or material dependence”.44 That trust is then monetized, as the AI “(hyper)nudges” the user toward consumer behaviors that “may not be in their interests, or even be harmful to their wellbeing”.44

This is the perfection of the “exploitative” model.4 It makes it “difficult for a user to distinguish a conversation with the AI assistant from interactions with a real person”.44 This, by definition, returns the entire adult population to the cognitive state of a child: in a trusted relationship with an entity, unable to detect its underlying persuasive intent. The 73% higher CTR 41 is empirical proof of this vulnerability.

Section 3.3: The “Manipulation Machine”: Generative AI and Scalable Deception#

If conversational AI is the relationship, generative AI is the weapon. It is the engine that creates the content for this mass, personalized manipulation. We are currently in the “wild west” period of this technology 45, with documented risks of “manipulative practices” 45, “deepfakes” that spread misinformation 46, algorithmic bias 47, and “deceptive AI outputs” or “hallucinations”.45

As described in 39, the future of advertising is one with “no big idea, no storyboard.” It is just “thousands of generative outputs” tested against “billions of consumer signals.” This is a “highly scalable ‘manipulation machine’” 29 that can target “individuals based on their unique vulnerabilities without requiring human input”.29

This model represents the end of accountability. An ad that “behaves more like a system than a message” 39 is an ad that cannot be regulated by traditional means. It fulfills the legal fear that ads will become “so shattered and distorted that [there is] little opportunity for political rivals, journalists, or even more scrutinizing citizens to rebut mistruths”.48 This is the technological “perfect crime”—a form of persuasion that is so personalized, so ephemeral, and so non-public that it cannot be reviewed, critiqued, or countered. It is the technological invalidation of all current regulatory frameworks.

Part IV: The Unhealthy Economy: Quantifying the Cost of an Ad-Based Society#

Section 4.1: The Engine of Sickness: The Scale of U.S. Advertising#

The query demands an investigation into “how much of the economy is supporting an unhealthy cycle.” This can be quantified by measuring the total economic weight of the U.S. advertising industry, which acts as the engine of this cycle.

According to 2024 and 2025 industry forecasts, the total media advertising spend in the United States is projected to reach $422 billion in 2025.15 The digital component of this is forecasted to be $324.9 billion.15 This expenditure is so vast that it equates to almost 1.5% of the total U.S. Gross Domestic Product.49 The “manipulation machine” (AI-driven programmatic advertising) is the fastest-growing component, reaching $134.8 billion in 2024, an 18% year-over-year increase.50

This $422 billion figure is not “economic activity” in a vacuum. It is, in effect, a tax. It is a $422 billion tax paid by consumers, who absorb this cost in the form of higher prices for all goods and services, to fund the very ecosystem of persuasion (Part I) and surveillance (Part II) designed to make them sicker, more anxious, and more pliable (Part I). This $422 billion is the annual cost of the “unhealthy cycle.”

Section 4.2: Paying for Freedom: The Aggregate Cost of an Ad-Free Life#

The second part of the query’s economic analysis is to estimate the “ransom” U.S. households would have to pay to “buy their freedom” from this ad-supported model. This calculation contrasts the ad-spend “tax” (Table 1) with the theoretical cost to opt-out.

1. Baseline Unit: There are approximately 132.2 million households in the United States as of 2024.52

2. Video Streaming “Ransom”: The average U.S. household subscribes to between 4 and 6 streaming services 54 and already spends between $51 and $109 per month on them.54 Many of these (like Amazon Prime Video, Disney+, Paramount+) have now adopted an ad-supported default. To upgrade a conservative bundle to be fully ad-free (e.g., Netflix Standard $17.99/mo, Disney+ Premium $18.99/mo, Paramount+ w/Showtime $12.99/mo 57), a household would pay a significant premium. A conservative estimate for a comprehensive, ad-free video bundle is ~$50.00 per household, per month.

3. Social Media “Ransom”: This is the newer, more explicit “ransom.”

  • There are ~246 million social media users in the U.S. 58, which averages to ~1.86 users per household.
  • The costs for ad-free subscriptions are:
    • YouTube Premium: $13.99/mo 59
    • X Premium (50% fewer ads): $8/mo 59
    • TikTok (testing): $4.99/mo 59
    • Meta “Subscription No Ads” (SNA) (Europe): ~$14/mo 60
  • The total cost per user for these four platforms is ~$40.98/month.
  • The total cost per household (1.86 users) is ~$76.22/month.

4. Total National “Ransom”:

  • Total per household: $50.00 (Video) + $76.22 (Social) = $126.22 per month.
  • Total annual cost per household: $1,514.64.
  • Total National “Ransom”: $1,514.64 * 132.2 million households = $199.94 Billion per year.

This analysis reveals two critical findings. First, the total “ransom” for Americans to buy back their privacy and attention (~$200 billion) is a staggering figure. But second, it is still less than half of the $422 billion 15 that the ad industry spends to capture their attention.

The $222 billion difference ($422B in ad spend minus $200B in ransom) represents the deadweight loss of the surveillance model. It is the cost of the “arms race”—the trackers, ad tech, data brokers, and psychological profiling—that provides zero value to the consumer.

Furthermore, the “Subscription No Ads” (SNA) model 60 being tested in Europe is a trap. It explicitly frames privacy and cognitive autonomy as a luxury good.62 This creates a two-tiered “sickness” society: the wealthy can afford the $1,515 annual “ransom” to buy their way out of the “unhealthy cycle,” while the poor and middle class—who are, as research shows, already more vulnerable to materialism and its related unhappiness 9—are forced to remain in the manipulation ecosystem. This is a regressive tax on well-being.

Section 4.3: Contentious Debates: Addressing the Counterarguments#

A rigorous analysis must also address the primary defenses of the advertising industry.

1. The “Economic Engine” Argument: The industry argues that advertising is a key economic driver, with one Deloitte study quantifying that 1 euro of ad spend generates 7 euros for the EU GDP.63 It is also credited with creating millions of jobs 63 and funding a “diverse, pluralistic media landscape” that is “free” to consumers.64

  • Rebuttal: This “free” media argument is a fallacy. As Table 1 demonstrates, the cost is hidden in the $422 billion “ad tax” that inflates the cost of all goods. The 7-to-1 GDP “benefit” 63 is a gross number, not a net one. It conveniently omits the massive, uncounted negative externalities—the billions in public health costs to treat the obesity 4, hypertension 5, and mental health crises 9 that the “unhealthy cycle” directly fuels.

2. The “Information” Argument: A philosophical defense of advertising posits that it is a “benevolent institution” 66 that provides “useful information” 26 to rational consumers.

  • Rebuttal: This is empirically false. As proven in Part I of this report, the modern advertising model is explicitly non-rational, “emotional” 6, and designed to “bypass rational consideration”.7 The “ads as information” school 67 is describing a theoretical model that has not reflected reality since, perhaps, the 1920s.

3. The “Causality” Argument: Industry proponents often claim that “blaming” advertising is too simplistic 68 and that critics cannot prove a causal link between ad exposure and harmful behavior, such as smoking initiation.69

  • Rebuttal: This is a disingenuous public relations tactic, not a scientific argument. As documented, public health 2 and experimental research 69 have established these causal links. This “causality” debate is a well-known disinformation campaign 2, pioneered by the tobacco industry 70, designed to “obscure their negative health impacts” 2 and “thwart regulation”.2

Part V: A Call to Action: The Case for Democratic Regulation#

Section 5.1: The Failure of Self-Regulation#

The U.S. advertising market is currently governed by a system of “self-regulation,” which the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) has often “applauded”.71 This system has been a documented failure.

Industry self-regulation initiatives, such as those for marketing junk food to children, have been evaluated and found to have resulted in “minimal improvements at best”.7 Basic compliance with FTC guidance on ad disclosures is “notably low”.73

The core of the problem is that the FTC is regulating the wrong thing. The Commission’s framework is based on a “free market” model where advertising’s role is to provide a “free flow of information”.74 This entire report has demonstrated that the modern advertising model is based on disinformation, emotional manipulation, and psychological exploitation, not information. The entire U.S. regulatory framework is obsolete because it is regulating advertising as it existed in 1950, not as it exists today.

Section 5.2: The First Amendment Fallacy#

The primary legal barrier to meaningful regulation is the First Amendment, which is used to shield both political and commercial advertising.

1. Political Ads: This is a “novel threat”.48 Because political speech is “staunchly protected,” it is “virtually impossible” to regulate or ban microtargeted political ads 48, which are “so shattered and distorted” that they cannot be rebutted by rivals or journalists.48

2. Commercial Ads: Advertising is protected by the “commercial speech doctrine,” which, as of a key 2011 analysis, rests on four “outdated and inadequate” premises 7: 1) that ads convey concrete info, 2) that recipients use this info to make rational decisions, 3) that misleading ads are distinguishable from others, and 4) that disclosures can cure any potential harm.7

As this report has proven, none of these premises are true in the era of AI-driven, non-rational, neurological advertising. The First Amendment is being used as a shield to protect a business model (surveillance capitalism 75) that is, in itself, destroying the “marketplace of ideas” 48 that the amendment was designed to protect.

The thesis of this report—that marketing must be democratically regulated—is therefore not an anti-First Amendment argument. It is a pro-First Amendment argument. To save the marketplace of ideas and restore the “cognitive autonomy” 44 of citizens, the commercial architecture of surveillance and manipulation that is poisoning the public sphere must be dismantled.

Section 5.3: Pathways to Mitigation (The Call to Action)#

The “unhealthy cycle” is a systemic, multi-faceted problem. Mitigating it requires a multi-pronged strategy that moves beyond failed self-regulation.

  1. Adopt a Public Health Framework (The Thesis)

The primary call to action is to “substitute… a public health framework for the currently dominant free market ideology”.2 Harmful marketing must be regulated as a public health vector, similar to environmental pollutants. This reframes the entire debate, moving it from the FTC (market information) to public health (safety and well-being).

  1. Democratically Regulate the “Manipulation Machine”

The most effective solution is to starve the AI-driven “manipulation machine” 29 of its fuel: data.

  • Limit Data Collection: Enact strong federal privacy legislation, similar to the proposed American Data Privacy and Protection Act (ADPPA), that “put[s] objective boundaries around the collection, use, and sharing of personal information”.75 This includes banning “surveillance” advertising models that track users across the web.75 This shifts the legal battleground from “speech” (where regulators lose 7) to “privacy” (where they can win).
  • Mandate Transparency: Require “enforceable regulatory harmonization” 77 and radical transparency for all advertising algorithms, data, and targeting, allowing for independent, third-party audits.48

3. Build Cognitive Resilience

  • Mandate Media Literacy: Implement comprehensive K-12 media literacy education, as states like California, Delaware, Illinois, and New Jersey have already begun to do.79 This is a “human-centered” 80 solution to give citizens the “critical thinking skill[s]” 81 needed to “develop independent judgments about media content”.81
  • Fund “Counter-Advertising”: Allocate public health funds to create and deploy “counter-ads” 70 that “challenge the dominant view” 70 and expose the harms of the processed food, alcohol, and other “unhealthy” industries.
  1. Rebuild the Public Sphere

The ad-based model only has such power because of the lack of alternatives.

  • Fund Alternatives: Citizens must “support public service media organizations and local news outlets”.82 Public policy must be designed to “rebuild local news” 84 and create ad-free “public spaces” 85 for democratic life.
  • Support Grassroots Action: This change will be fought by the $422 billion ad lobby. Citizens must mobilize and support grassroots movements 86 like S.T.O.P. (Surveillance Technology Oversight Project) 87 that are “fighting excessive local and state-level surveillance”.87

This is a public health crisis. The “unhealthy cycle” is not a personal failing; it is a political and economic architecture. It was built by a century of commercial choices, and it can only be dismantled by a new generation of democratic ones.
